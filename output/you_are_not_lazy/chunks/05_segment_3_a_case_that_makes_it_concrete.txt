Think about the last time you avoided something simple. Not a huge project — something small. A phone call. A form. One email.

If it were really about laziness, effort should predict avoidance — the harder the task, the more you'd put it off. But that's not usually what happens. People routinely avoid five-minute tasks for weeks. What predicts avoidance far better than difficulty is how the task makes you *feel* — boring, humiliating if you get it wrong, ambiguous about where to even start.

Researchers Pychyl and Fuschia Sirois have identified the specific emotions most likely to trigger procrastination: boredom, frustration, resentment, anxiety, and self-doubt. Not "this is hard." More like "this makes me feel something I don't want to feel."

That reframes almost everything. The tax return isn't being avoided because it's difficult — a surgeon can clearly handle difficult. It's being avoided because it might involve confusion, or a mistake, or a feeling of incompetence in an area completely outside her expertise. The emotion is the obstacle, not the task itself.
