Here's the definition researchers actually use: procrastination is voluntarily delaying a task despite knowing you'll probably be worse off for it.

Notice what's built into that definition — you *know* it's a bad idea. You're not confused about the consequences. You're not unaware the deadline is real. You do it anyway.

That's the detail that breaks the "laziness" theory immediately. Laziness would mean not caring. But most people who procrastinate care *intensely* — they're anxious about the task, they think about it constantly, they just can't seem to start it. That's not indifference. That's something else entirely.

Professor Timothy Pychyl of Carleton University, one of the leading procrastination researchers, has a term for what's really going on: short-term mood repair. You're not avoiding the task. You're avoiding the *feeling* the task creates in you, right now, in this moment.
