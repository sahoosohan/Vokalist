# Why Behind Us — Video Script (12-Minute Version)
## "The People Who Can't Picture Anything In Their Head"
Target length: ~12 minutes | ~1,800 words | 130–160 wpm pacing

---

### 1. THE HOOK (0:00–0:15)

Close your eyes for a second and picture an apple.

Red skin. A little stem. Maybe a bite taken out of it.

Some of you just saw that clearly, almost like a photo. But some of you — right now — saw nothing. No apple. No color. No image at all. Just… blackness, or the *idea* of an apple with nothing to look at.

And until a few years ago, most of those people had no idea that made them different.

---

### 2. SETUP / STAKES (0:15–0:45)

It's called aphantasia — the inability to voluntarily create mental images. No picture-thinking at all.

By the end of this video, you'll know exactly which type of mind you have, why science only discovered this condition had a name less than a decade ago, why some of the most visual professions on Earth are run by people who can't picture anything, and what's happening at the *opposite* extreme of the same spectrum.

---

### 3. MAIN CONTENT

#### Segment 1 — What aphantasia actually is (0:45–2:30)

Most people assume imagination works the same way for everyone. You think of your childhood home, and a picture appears in your head — walls, furniture, the smell of the kitchen, all of it rendered like a movie you're replaying.

For people with aphantasia, none of that happens. Ask them to picture their mother's face, and they can tell you facts about it — she has brown eyes, a small scar above her eyebrow — but there's no image. No visual. It's pure information, no picture attached.

It's not blindness. It's not a lack of imagination in the emotional sense — these people can still be creative, still dream, still recognize faces perfectly well when they're actually looking at them. What's missing is the *voluntary* visual replay function. The mental screen just isn't there.

Researchers test for this using something called the Vividness of Visual Imagery Questionnaire — a simple survey that asks you to rate, on a scale, how clearly you can picture things like a sunrise, a familiar street, or a friend's face. Score low enough across enough categories, and you land in aphantasia territory. It's subjective, but paired with brain imaging, it's become the standard way researchers sort people onto the spectrum.

And here's the strange part: most people with aphantasia don't find out until adulthood, often by accident — because when someone says "picture this," they assumed everyone meant it as a figure of speech.

#### Segment 2 — The science behind it (2:30–4:30)

The term "aphantasia" was only coined in 2015, by neurologist Adam Zeman at the University of Exeter. Before that, there wasn't even a word for it — which meant there was no way to Google it, no way to know you weren't alone.

Zeman's research pointed to something happening in the connection between the frontal cortex — the planning, decision-making part of your brain — and the visual cortex, which processes images. In most people, when you "imagine" something, your frontal cortex sends a kind of request down to the visual cortex, which lights up almost like it's actually seeing the thing. It's a weaker signal than real vision, but it's there.

In people with aphantasia, brain scans show that request either isn't being sent the same way, or the visual cortex isn't responding to it. The eyes work fine. The visual cortex works fine when actually looking at something. It's the *internal* signal — the one that fires when you close your eyes and try to conjure something from memory — that seems to go quiet.

Some researchers have also measured something called the pupillary light reflex — your pupils naturally shrink slightly when you imagine something bright, like a sunny sky, even with your eyes closed. In people with strong visual imagery, that reflex shows up reliably. In people with aphantasia, it barely moves at all — which gives scientists a physical, measurable signal to work with instead of just self-reported descriptions.

Estimates suggest somewhere between 1 and 4 percent of people have some degree of aphantasia. That means if you've got a few hundred subscribers to this channel, some of them are watching this and realizing, for the first time, why they always struggled with those "visualize your goals" exercises.

#### Segment 3 — A case that makes it concrete (4:30–6:30)

One of the clearest examples comes from Blake Ross, a co-founder of Firefox. He wrote a widely shared essay describing the moment he realized, in his thirties, that when people said "picture a beach," they meant it literally. He had spent his entire life assuming it was a metaphor — that nobody actually *saw* anything, they just meant "think about a beach."

He described trying to picture his own daughter's face and getting nothing. Not darkness — just an absence, the same way you don't see anything behind your own head. He could describe her perfectly from memory. He just couldn't *see* her.

What's fascinating is that people with aphantasia often perform completely normally on tasks that seem like they'd require visualization — because their brains route around it. Instead of seeing an image, some of them describe knowing facts about the thing, in a kind of pure conceptual form, no picture required. Some even test normally on spatial reasoning tasks, like mentally rotating a shape, despite reporting zero visual experience while doing it — which tells researchers that "imagining" and "seeing an image" might be two separate systems entirely.

### MID-VIDEO RE-HOOK (~6:00)

But here's the part that breaks most people's assumptions.

If picturing things is impossible for them — how do some of the best visual artists in the world have aphantasia? And on the complete opposite end, what does it look like when someone's imagination is *too* vivid?

#### Segment 4 — The twist, and the opposite extreme (6:30–8:30)

Glen Keane, the Disney animator behind Ariel and the Beast, has spoken about having little to no visual imagery in his mind. Neither did the illustrator Ed Catmull, a co-founder of Pixar. These are people whose entire careers are built on visual imagination — and by the strict definition, they can't "see" anything when they close their eyes.

The current theory is that visualization and creativity aren't actually the same brain function. Creativity might rely more on conceptual thinking — combining ideas, understanding structure, knowing what "looks right" — than on literally rendering a picture first. Some researchers think people with aphantasia may even rely *more* heavily on that structural, conceptual reasoning, precisely because they never had the image to lean on.

Now flip to the other end of the spectrum: hyperphantasia. These are people whose mental imagery is so vivid it can be almost indistinguishable from actually seeing something. Ask them to picture a beach, and they don't just get an impression — they get detail. Individual grains of sand. The exact shade of the water. Some describe it as closer to watching a high-definition video than "imagining" anything.

Interestingly, hyperphantasia seems to appear at roughly similar rates to aphantasia — meaning both extremes are rare, and most people sit somewhere comfortably in the middle without ever questioning it. Some researchers believe hyperphantasia may be linked to higher rates of vivid dreaming and even certain anxiety patterns, since intrusive mental images can be just as vivid as pleasant ones. Nobody's fully mapped why some brains land on one extreme, the other, or the middle — that part is still being researched.

#### Segment 5 — Why this matters to you (8:30–10:30)

So why does any of this matter if you're not one of the 1 to 4 percent?

Because it changes how you think about advice like "just visualize your success" or "picture your worst fear to overcome it." For years, that kind of language was treated as universal — something anyone could do if they just tried harder. For a meaningful chunk of the population, it was never a matter of trying harder. The mental hardware for that specific task simply works differently.

It also reframes a lot of everyday communication. When a teacher says "picture the water cycle," or a therapist says "imagine yourself in that safe place," they're assuming a shared experience that a noticeable slice of any room genuinely doesn't have. None of that makes the advice useless — it just means the instruction lands completely differently depending on which end of the spectrum someone's on.

And there's a bigger question researchers are still chasing: does this spectrum affect memory itself, not just imagination? Some early studies suggest people with aphantasia may recall fewer visual details of past events, even though their factual memory is just as strong. Meanwhile, people with hyperphantasia sometimes report memories so vivid they're hard to distinguish from imagination after enough time passes. If that holds up in future research, it means this isn't just about picturing an apple — it's about how differently two people can experience their own past.

### 4. THE PAYOFF (10:30–11:15)

So the next time someone casually says "just picture it" — understand that they might be describing something as automatic as breathing, something rendered in near-photographic detail, or something a meaningful slice of the population has never once experienced in their life.

Same instruction. Three completely different internal experiences. And until 2015, we didn't even have a word to describe any of it.
