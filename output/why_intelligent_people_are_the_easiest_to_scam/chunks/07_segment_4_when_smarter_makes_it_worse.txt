Researchers studying scam susceptibility have identified something called the "sunk cost trap" combined with analytical overconfidence. Once a smart person has invested real time analyzing a scheme — reading the documents, running the numbers, forming a reasoned judgment — they become *more* resistant to warnings from others, not less. They reason: "I already looked at this closely. I'm not an idiot. I would have caught red flags."

This creates a strange effect where the more effort someone puts into evaluating a scam, the more committed they become to their own conclusion — even when that conclusion is wrong. Psychologists call this escalation of commitment, and it applies as strongly to a PhD as it does to anyone else. Arguably more so, because a PhD has more practice defending well-reasoned positions and more identity tied up in being the person who gets things right.

Scammers targeting high-net-worth or highly educated victims have reportedly adapted their scripts specifically to exploit this — presenting information in a way that flatters the target's intelligence, framing the "opportunity" as something only a sophisticated investor would understand or qualify for. The scam doesn't just steal money. It recruits the victim's own intelligence as the weapon used against them.
