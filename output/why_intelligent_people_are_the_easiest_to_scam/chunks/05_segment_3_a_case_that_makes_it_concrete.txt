Consider the case pattern seen repeatedly in what's called the "pig butchering" scam — a long-con investment fraud that built trust over weeks or months before asking for money. Victims described the scammers as patient, articulate, and detail-oriented. The scam worked precisely because it didn't feel like a scam. It felt like due diligence.

Educated professionals were frequently drawn in because the scam *mimicked* the legitimate research process they were used to — data points, screenshots of "returns," terminology that matched real financial jargon. A less financially literate person might have been intimidated and asked someone else. A financially literate person often felt equipped to evaluate it themselves, and trusted that evaluation.

That's the core irony: the same expertise that should have been protective became the thing that kept them engaged long enough for the trap to close.
