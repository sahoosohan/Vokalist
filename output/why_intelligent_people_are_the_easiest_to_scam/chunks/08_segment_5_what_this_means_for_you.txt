So what actually protects people, if intelligence doesn't?

Research on fraud resistance points less toward raw intelligence and more toward specific habits: a default skepticism toward urgency, a rule of always involving a second person before financial decisions above a certain threshold, and treating strong emotional pressure — excitement or fear — as itself a red flag, regardless of how convincing the surrounding facts seem.

The uncomfortable takeaway is this: the belief "I'm too smart to fall for this" is itself one of the most reliable predictors of vulnerability, because it's the thing that stops people from applying the same scrutiny they'd apply to something they felt less confident about. The people best protected aren't the smartest people in the room — they're the people humble enough to still ask "could I be wrong here?" even after they've done the analysis.

That's a learnable habit, not a fixed trait. Which means the actual defense isn't more intelligence. It's building a specific kind of doubt into your process, on purpose, every time — especially when you feel most certain.
