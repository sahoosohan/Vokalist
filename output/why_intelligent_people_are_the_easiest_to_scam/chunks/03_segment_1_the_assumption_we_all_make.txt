Ask most people who's most likely to fall for a scam, and they'll describe someone else — someone older, less educated, less "with it" than themselves. It's a comforting assumption. It's also wrong.

Fraud researchers have found that victims span every education and income bracket, and in several documented cases, higher-education professionals were disproportionately targeted and successfully scammed in investment fraud specifically — precisely because they were confident enough in their own judgment to skip the caution a less-confident person might apply.

This isn't a fringe finding. It shows up again and again in behavioral economics research: intelligence measures your ability to process information. It says almost nothing about your ability to recognize when you're being manipulated. Those are two different skills, and scammers only need to defeat one of them.
